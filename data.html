Step 2: Compile Data

  Overview

Since MSP is often multidisciplinary in nature, there can be substantial spatial and non-spatial data requirements. For coastal and marine spatial planning, common data requirements include biophysical and oceanographic information, such as elevation, maps of human activities and socioeconomic characteristics in the coastal zone, and data on the distribution and quality of coastal and marine habitats. When valuation is a part of the assessment, economic data is also needed, which can include market prices, property values, and discount rates. An ecosystem service approach to MSP can help to focus on the specific data requirements to minimize conflicts and maximizing the benefits people obtain from healthy ecosystems.

 

  Outputs

  • Preliminary spatial layers that can be refined through iterative analysis and synthesis (early)
  • Working database to use as inputs to MSP decision-support tools (middle of project)
  • Final database to share with local public and broader community (conclusion)
 

  Tips

  • Don’t spend too much time initially on this step
  • Use global/regional datasets to get a 'lay of the land' and identify data gaps
  • Show preliminary results to experts and stakeholder (e.g., scientists, local NGOs); use feedback to refine information (Iterate step)
  • If funding permits, consider adding a dedicated GIS/data analyst to your team
 

  Global Databases

We organize global MSP datasets into the following thematic categories. Click on the name to visit each website.

  1. Biophysical - Physiographic, oceanographic, climatological, and biological
  2. Cultural - Geopolitical, human use activities and infrustructure, socio-economic
  3. Remotely-sensed - Data aquired using earth observation technology (e.g., satellite imagery, LiDAR)
  • UNEP Ocean Data Viewer (1,2) Data for the conservation of marine and coastal biodiversity

  • MarineExplore.org (1,2) The world's ocean data at your fingertips; access measurements and create datasets in minutes
  • Reefs at Risk Database (1) A global database of coral reefs and threats from a wide range of human activities
  • Blue Habitats (1) Home to the global seafloor geomorphic features map
  • OpenFisheries.org (1) A platform for aggregating global fishery data
  • Worldpop (2) High resolution, contemporary data on human population distributions
  • USGS Global Visualization Viewer (3) A global, searchable database of historical Landsat imagery
  • MODIS (1,3) Products from a variety of disciplines, including oceanography, biology, and atmospheric science

      Helper Tools


    Pre-processing spatial and tabular data is a challenging but necessary step before these information can be plugged into InVEST or other decision-support tools. NatCap has created helper tools and documentation (or "on-ramps") to help InVEST users more easily prepare and format inputs for different models:
    1. InVEST 3.x Data Requirements - A sortable spreadsheet listing the required and optional inputs for each InVEST model
    2. InVEST 3.1.0 User's Guide - Every chapter includes a "Data Needs" section outlining input and formatting requirements for each model
    3. Marine InVEST Helper Tools - A GIS toolbox for pre- and post-processing data to be used with InVEST models (requires ArcGIS 10.x license)
    4. Experimental Software Tools - A discussion thread where NatCap staff and members of the broader community share details about new helper tools

     

      Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

    Q: Where is the best place to find data?
    A: We have compiled a list of the global and regional MSP databases we use most often (see above). Local or regional government agencies often have data available upon request, if they don’t have an existing data portal. Be clear with partners about data needs; each partner and collaborator is likely to have a network of potential data sources.

    Q: How much is enough data?
    A: Revisit the project objectives and methodologies outlined during the scoping process. Start with just enough data to do preliminary analyses (e.g., run a selected tool). It is often easier to add and refine data as you go rather than waiting for it.
