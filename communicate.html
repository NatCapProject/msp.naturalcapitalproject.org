Step 6: Communicate (Inform Decisions)

  Overview

At this step in the process, the team has done significant work together and it is crucial to get the word out about it in ways that resonate with decision-makers and stakeholders. Careful targeting of particular audiences should drive the creation of products. Opportunities to present results to decision-makers should be created, seized, and thoughtfully delivered (E.G. PRESENTATION ON METRICS TO THE WCA BOARD - ANYTHING TO CITE SPENCER?). If well-coordinated, reports detailing the results can be incorporated directly into planning documents (CITE BZ PLAN, IRWM PLAN FOR SANTA CRUZ). In some instances, it is important to publish peer-reviewed papers to help establish the credibility of the science (CITE BZ PNAS AND HRA PAPERS; LANGRIDGE ET AL.) and thus gain the support and trust of internal science advisors and the broader scientific community. Also, communication with stakeholders can be key: simple diagrams and interactive maps can allow non-technical users to explore and understand results (CITE BZ WEB PORTAL, NATURE CLIMATE CHANGE PAPER INTERACTIVE MAP). Appropriate communication can lead to transparency, a shared understanding among diverse actors, and an increased likelihood of action.


 

  Goals

  • Create syntheses of the results (e.g, reports, executive summaries, peer-reviewed papers, presentations, interactive maps, etc.) tailored to intended audiences
  • Share findings—and the ways in which they can inform planning and action—with the broader community
 

  Tips

  • Use appropriate media to communicate findings to different audiences (e.g., technical report for scientists vs. executive summary for government officials).
  • Communication is two-way; be a good listener and develop products that meet the needs of your audience.
  • Don’t use jargon (including, in most instances, the phrase “ecosystem services”. Use the terms that make sense to your stakeholders and decision-makers.
  • Tell stories. Human brains are wired to look for and remember a narrative. Find the stories and tell them well.
  • A central challenge here is that we are working to make implicit connections explicit. Don’t leave people with the message that “it is complicated”. Rather, leave them with the message that “we can make better decisions if we think this through differently”
  • Be honest about the science and underlying uncertainty (A GOOD REF FOR CITING UNCERTAINTY?).
  • Consider building an online repository (e.g., mapping portal, data download) to archive and dissementate spatial data.
  • Organizing a workshop to share results can be a good mechanism for feedback, drive the future evolution of the work (see “iteration”), and increase buy-in.

  Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What kinds of information do decision-makers want to hear?
A: Decision-making is exceedingly complex. People have to balance multiple objectives, and need to gain the support of their stakeholders. For the most part, they want information that they can readily connect to the decisions at-hand. Thus, it is essential to involve those you hope to influence in your process from the beginning, so that you know how to connect your results to their needs. When co-created, information is more likely to be legitimate, credible, and salient (CITE CASH AND MCKENZIE ET AL.).

Q: How should I present results?
A: Because the process of MSP relies on spatial information, maps are an obvious choice for communicating results, especially spatial differences throughout a region. However, maps can be difficult to synthesize and to compare. Simple diagrams using arrows, bars, or graphs can help visualize ecosystem service synergies and trade-offs across scenarios (see some examples in the gallery). Belize

Communicating and applying results in Belize was multi-faceted, including a formal planning document, executive summary, peer-reviewed publications and online maps, videos and brochures. All synthesis products and communication materials were tailored to specific audiences and decision-makers.



  • The Belize Integrated Coastal Zone Management Plan (ICZMP) offers a legal framework for implementing a spatial zoning plan to ensure a sustainable future for Belize's coasts and oceans supported by thriving local communities and a vibrant economy.
  • Peer-reviewed publications can build credibility within the scientific community.
  • Online analytical tools provided clear, targeted information to planners, reserve managers, and other decision-makers in Belize without requiring technical expertise.
  • This online mapping portal enabled citizens of Belize to explore zoning recommendations in greater detail than the static maps included within the ICZM plan.
