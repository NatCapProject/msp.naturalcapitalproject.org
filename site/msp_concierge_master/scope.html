Step 1: Scope

  Overview

Scoping is essential for framing appropriate scientific questions, improving the quality of analytical outputs, and increasing the likelihood that results are salient and accessible to stakeholders and policymakers. It can also create the conditions that enable success by clarifying the policy question and ecosystem services of interest, identifying local leaders and champions, and determining whether a policy window has opened up or could open as the result of the research.


 

  Goals

  • Clear understanding of the scientific or policy questions to be answered
  • Project team with clear roles and responsibilities
  • Assessment and synthesis of relevant literature (e.g., literature review)
  • Work plan, including expected methodology and timeline
  • Preliminary assessment of available and needed data (e.g., gap analysis)
 

  Tips

This step serves to link the decisions, decision-makers, and scientific support staff to improve the quality of scientific outputs and ensure that results are salient, accessible and designed to meet policy needs. We find that scoping is most successful in driving action when it includes several essential tasks:
  • Defining partnerships, roles and objectives
  • Setting a work plan
  • Identifying key decision questions
  • Selecting methods and tools for analysis
  • Establishing a process to engage stakeholders and exchange knowledge
  • Developing a rapport and genuine respect between partners
  • Ensuring plans are feasible given timelines and available resources
  • Identifying essential expertise (e.g., planner, project manager, GIS tech) and for which phases these skills are required

      Sample MSP Scoping Questions

    • Is there a specific problem(s) people are rallying around?
    • Which coastal and ocean sectors are most relevant to the planning process?
    • How can an ecosystem service approach aid marine spatial planning?
    • What are the essential considerations and trade-offs to be considered for the decision at hand?
    • Who is at the table? Which sectors, stakeholders and decision-makers should be engaged?
     

      Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

    Q: Why is scoping important? Is it okay to start scoping later in the process?
    A: Early scoping saves time down the road. By clearly outlining project objectives, scoping focuses efforts on key questions and ensures that the planning process will yield the high impact results. Working with stakeholders and decision-makers from the beginning can save time (both yours and theirs). During this scoping step, we have found that issues we thought mattered, don't always matter to local people. For example, only sometimes do they want to see valuation. Also, sometimes the ecosystem and economic measures we used in one location were not relevant to a decision-making in another.

    Q: How much scoping is enough?
    A: Scoping should match the scale of the project. Scoping can start with a brief description of work (e.g., report), but may end up being a 2-3 month process (see our Barbados example). Remember that scoping happens in stages and is typically part of an iterative process. Data limitations, combined with the need for further scenario development and synthesis of initial results, may require additional literature review, new partners, or the need for refined questions.

    Barbados

    To aid in a coastal management plan for Barbados, NatCap worked with key organizational partners to create a formal literature review and gap analysis. The report outlined the provisioning, regulating, and cultural ecosystem services of interest and identified threats to these services. The project team then collected, reviewed, and synthesized available literature and spatial data. In addition to this synthesis, preliminary runs of select InVEST models were used to identify data gaps. The literature review and data gap assessment not only organized key information, but also served as a method to gain consensus about priority ecosystem services in Barbados and the steps needed to complete a marine spatial plan.
