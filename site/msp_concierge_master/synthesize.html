Step 5: Synthesize

  Overview

Outputs produced by decision-support tools are not always in a format for immediate use. The "Synthesize" step serves to make sense of raw model outputs so that the most pertinent information can be included in the decision-making process. Synthesis helps visualize and compare different ecosystem service outcomes in space by highlighting synergies and trade-offs. This step typically requires substantial "post-processing" of model outputs. Examples of post-processing include normalizing outputs across multiple ecosystem services or objectives, conversion to local monetary units, or producing maps showing percent change from an established baseline (e.g., current scenario).


 

  Goals

  • Refine raw outputs produced during the Analyze step
  • Identify key metrics and information to be included in the communicate and apply phase (Communicate step)
 

  Tips

  • Synthesis products such as maps, charts and figures should be clear and easy to interpret
  • Avoid using jargon, complex terminology or including superfluous information that will confuse your audience
  • Use simple metrics and visuals that make sense and seem to resonate with decision-makers
  • Ask a colleague or friend (who is not familiar with your project) to review synthesis products and offer feedback
  • Visit our Gallery to see examples of MSP visualization techniques

 

  Helper Tools


Synthesizing outputs from decision-support tools can be challenging. NatCap has created helper tools (or "off-ramps") that enable InVEST users to quickly explore outputs and perform basic post-processing. The following are links to examples of InVEST helper tools:
  1. InVIEW is a web app that automates common synthesis and visualization tasks that may be necessary after running InVEST. The app displays results of your latest model run in a web browser and allows users to easily explore and extract meaning from raw outputs without requiring a desktop GIS. Try it now with InVEST Coastal Vulnerability or Recreation model results. With InVIEW, users can:
    •  View InVEST outputs on a fully interactive, Google Earth-style, map
    •  Explore spatial patterns (e.g., ecosystem service hotspots)
    •  Compare scenarios and highlight changes over time
    •  Quickly diagnose issues and refine InVEST model inputs
  2. InVEST 3.1.0 User's Guide - Each chapter includes an "Interpreting Results" section that defines intermediate and final outputs of each model
  3. Identifying Opportunities for Climate-Compatible Tourism Development in Belize
    This online analytics tool enables users to summarize results from the InVEST coastal vulnerability model through a simple website. Model outputs are linked to 2010 population census and tourism expenditure data and then visualized as maps and graphs.
  4. Marine InVEST Helper Tools - A GIS toolbox for pre- and post-processing data to be used with InVEST models (requires ArcGIS 10.x license)

  Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: Is it possible to weigh options and compare tradeoffs if results are not in the same units?
A: We make tradeoffs all the time where we know the cost of something and a physical measure of what we get for that cost. First, try to synthesize results in a way that conveys to your audience what is being gained and/or lost. Quantify it ecologically (biophysical units) and economically (if possible) and spell out what can be gained through some management or policy action. After presenting this information (Communicate step) let the stakeholders and decision-makers decide. Monetizing outputs solely for the sake of producing results in a common currency may actually result in less informed decisions.

Q: Do decision-makers always want ecosystem service valuation?
A: No. It's not always about the money. Other metrics about livelihoods (e.g., number of jobs created) and ecological outcomes can be just as valuable to decision-makers.

Q: How do I synthesize tradeoffs among ecosystem services?
A: Tradeoffs should be synthesized with your project objectives and audience in mind. When multiple services can be represented using a common unit or currency (e.g., $, number of jobs, etc.), this unit may be summarized across scenarios. In cases where outputs have different units (e.g., hectares of habitat, pounds of fish, number of visitors, etc.), something as simple as proportional arrows can be used to represent increase or decrease across scenarios and services. See our Gallery for some synthesis ideas.

Q: Which synthesis tool(s) should I use for MSP?
A: Try synthesizing results using methods or tools that are familiar and available to your team. Members of NatCap have found it helpful to synthesize InVEST results using a combination of GIS (ArcGIS or QGIS) and design software like Adobe Illustrator or Microsoft PowerPoint. Open-source software like OpenOffice, R, or Python can also be used to produce synthesis products. Bottom line: there are no rules or restrictions here - be creative!

Vancouver Island

NatCap worked with the West Coast Aquatic Management Board (WCA), a public-private partnership among government agencies and diverse local stakeholders, in its creation of spatial plans for Barkley and Clayoquot Sounds on the west coast of Vancouver Island. We iteratively mapped and analyzed ecosystem services across realistic zoning scenarios to help decision-makers accommodate varied priorities for using marine resources. Local zoning maps provided realistic scenarios for coastal populations. For larger-scale planning questions, WCA used outputs from InVEST and other tools to compare a wide range of value metrics across different management scenarios.



  • Arrow diagrams highlight change across different units and goals within a marine spatial plan.
  • Flower diagrams can synthesize outcomes across multiple scenarios (C, S1, S2) and project goals (1-6), but may require additional explanation when presented to some audiences.
  • This map shows where proposed clear-cutting of trees will impact views along kayaking routes. Preservation of pristine recreational views is one metric that our partner, West Coast Aquatic, included in their planning goal of "vibrant communities, recreation and culture".
  • Spider plots visualize ES synergies and tradeoffs across scenarios (baseline, industry expansion, conservation), but can be difficult for some audiences to interpret.




Belize

To inform the design of the Integrated Coastal Zone Management Plan for Belize, we modeled three ecosystem services and produced biophysical and economic outputs for each: 1) catch and revenue from spiny lobster, 2) land protected and avoided damages from storms, and 3) visitation and expenditures from tourism. We assessed outcomes for these three services for the Current, Conservation, Informed Management and Development scenarios. National results for the entire country and each of the nine planning regions were produced and synthesized.



  • The InVEST habitat risk assessment model was used to understand differences in the cumulative risk to coral reefs, mangroves, and seagrass beds under three alternative zoning configurations.
  • Through its contribution to outdoor recreation, the environment provides services to people. We used the InVEST recreation & tourism model to map the spread of recreation by tourists.
  • A spiny lobster model for Belize was used to explore how ES returns from the fishery would respond to changes in lobster habitat (seagrass, mangrove, coral reef) or fishing pressure.
  • We used the InVEST coastal protection model to quantify the protective benefits that natural habitats provide against erosion and inundation in nearshore environments.
