Step 4: Analyze (Assess Outcomes)

  Overview

The "Analyze" step involves assessing ecosystem service outcomes by modeling how change in ecosystems and socio-economic conditions under alternative future scenarios leads to change in environmental benefits and values for people. Results can be spatially explicit and, when possible, should be directly tied to local beneficiaries (i.e., demand for those benefits) and reported using metrics that will resonate with stakeholders and policy-makers.


 

  Goals

  • Produce early outputs such as maps or summary tables to characterize the current distribution of ecosystem services (ES)
  • Using a stakeholder-driven process to compile data and develop scenarios, assess and compare future ES outcomes
 

  Tips

  • Before applying any decison-support tool make sure to understand its underlying assumptions and limitations
  • When deciding which tool(s) to use, examine existing use-cases where the tool has already been applied
  • Explore our compilation of NatCap MSP use-cases (below) to find a tool that suits your project needs
 

  MSP Decision-Support Tools and Use-Cases

Below we list some freely-available MSP analysis toolkits. Click on the tool's name to visit its website or click on the sub-bullet links to learn how the tool was applied as part of an MSP use-case.

  1. AIRES
    • Madagascar - ARIES marine ecosystem services models were developed for Madagascar to study coastal storm regulation and subsistence fisheries. Both of these services are critical in Madagascar due to its reliance on fisheries for protein and its susceptibility to tropical storms forming in the southwest Indian Ocean.
  2. Atlantis
    • Northern Gulf of California (Mexico) - Atlantis was used to estimate the benefits of compliance with existing fisheries regulations in the Northern Gulf of California, including spatial management, seasonal closures, gear restrictions, removal of illegal fishing, buyouts and programs to encourage gear switching, and the combination of these regulations into ‘full enforcement’.
  3. Coastal Resilience
    • Gulf of Mexico - With its high incidence of storms and hurricanes and valuable ecological and economic resources, the Gulf region is a high-risk area with great potential to demonstrate natural risk reduction solutions. The Coastal Resilience approach and tools have been applied Gulf-wide and at specific sites, including those that help identify where to implement oyster reef restoration to meet social and ecological goals.
  4. Cumulative Impacts
    • California Current - Researchers applied methods developed to map cumulative impacts globally to the California Current using more comprehensive and higher-quality data for 25 human activities and 19 marine ecosystems.
  5. Marine InVEST
    • Two InVEST MSP use-cases are described at the bottom of this page.
  6. MIMES
    • Mississippi Delta - Wetlands, Hurricanes and the Economy: The Value of Restoring the Mississippi River Delta
  7. WRI’s Valuation Methods and Tools
    • Caribbean - WRI’s valuation methods and tools help policymakers, civil society or other interested parties to assess the value to their economies of goods and services provided by coral reefs and to aid in setting coastal management policies. WRI offers tools for tourism/recreation, fisheries, and MPAs.

  Frequently Asked Questions (FAQ)

Q: What guidance is available to help me choose from all the different decision-support tools for MSP?
A: In 2012 the Center for Ocean Solutions and PacMARA released a Decision Guide: Selecting decision support tools for marine spatial planning

Q: Which metrics are most effective for highlighting future change in the distribution and delivery of ecosystem services to people?
A: It depends largely on what ecosystem service and function are under consideration. Stakeholder engagement can elucidate the source(s) of environmental change, how these changes affect ecosystem function, and what ecological outputs will change. Once these connections become clear, it can then be useful to talk about metrics like economic valuation. This process of working from change, through function, and then to ecological outcomes to people is typically more important than any final measures of ecosystem services or value.

Q: How exactly can InVEST models be used to facilitate the MSP process?
A: Visit our Marine InVEST page to learn about the models -or- read through the NatCap use-case summaries at the bottom of this and subsequent pages to learn how different models in the InVEST toolkit were applied for MSP.

Q: If I have questions about using InVEST models or run into problems, where can I get help?
A: First, check the InVEST user’s guide chapter that describes your particular model of interest. Each chapter covers the theory behind the model as well as detailed information about data formatting requirements and how to interpret the results (model outputs). Within InVEST, each model includes sample input data that offers a template for how input data should be formatted. Also, the NatCap User Help Forum allows users to converse with other InVEST users and NatCap staff.

  Use-Cases

California

The InVEST coastal vulnerability model was used to identify where coastal ecosystems reduce the number of people exposed to storms and future sea level rise along the California coast. These areas were further analyzed at the county level to highlight which habitats play the biggest role in reducing numbers of people at risk and thus where to prioritize conservation of these ecosystems. This early information provided local agencies with a process to better determine how their coasts and oceans can be managed more sustainably. Additional InVEST models are now being applied to map the full suite of benefits provided by coastal-marine ecosystems, including recreation opportunities, scenic quality and watershed services.

Belize

A risk assessment was conducted in coastal Belize to map impacts to habitats from eight coastal-marine human uses. Based on the amount of "functional" habitat (healthy ecosystems that are capable of providing benefits to people), three InVEST models were applied to map, measure and value future ecosystem service delivery, including coastal protection, fisheries, and tourism / recreation. Model results were used to illustrate where human activities pose the greatest risk to ecosystems and the resulting changes in ecosystem service values (both biophysical and economic).

  • Spatial representations of risk to three habitats were used to assess ecosystem service outcomes across the nine coastal planning regions of Belize
